The shift from policy to mechanism
NYDFS DFS guidance — 2023 amendment
Regulators now distinguish between a policy and a mechanism. A policy can be ignored. A mechanism cannot.
For years, documented access control policies were accepted as sufficient evidence of compliance. The 2023 NYDFS Part 500 amendments mark a structural shift. DFS now requires that privileged access controls be technically enforced — meaning the control operates independent of human discretion. A policy that says "no single vendor should hold unilateral access" is not compliant. An architecture that makes unilateral access technically impossible is.
What policy-based compliance looks like
Documented procedures for access review. Annual attestations. PAM solutions that vault credentials on behalf of the customer. Self-reported evidence to examiners. Controls that depend on employees and vendors following the policy correctly every time.
Policy-dependent
What mechanism-based compliance looks like
Architecture that makes unilateral access technically impossible. No stored credential to bypass. Every privileged action requires a live quorum from independent devices. Audit evidence produced by the access mechanism itself — not a separate logging layer.

Key sections addressed by SplitSecure
§500.7
Access privileges and management
Requires technically limiting access rights to only what is necessary. Prohibits shared admin accounts for privileged access. Requires periodic review and immediate revocation. SplitSecure enforces this at the credential layer — no shared secrets, no persistent admin credentials, every action requires quorum approval.

NYDFS guidance on third-party risk — October 2025
NYDFS Industry Letter — October 21, 2025
DFS guidance specifically flags MSPs, outsourced help desks, and insurance claims managers as highest-risk vendor categories.
NYDFS's October 2025 guidance on managing risks from third-party IT providers identifies managed service providers, outsourced help desk services, and insurance claims management as among the highest-risk vendor categories — specifically those with a high degree of system-level access and the ability to access sensitive data. The guidance emphasizes that covered entities are responsible for controls on these vendors' access to their systems, regardless of contractual language. SplitSecure addresses this directly: no vendor in any of these categories can hold a complete credential or act on a covered entity's systems without a live quorum of independent approvals from the covered entity's own devices.

Common compliance gaps
Where most DFS-regulated entities fall short of Part 500's requirements
DFS examinations increasingly focus on the gap between documented policies and technically enforced controls. These are the patterns examiners are finding — and the architectural responses that close them.
Gap patterns identified in DFS examinations
High risk
Vendor PAM holds complete credentials on behalf of covered entity
Most PAM solutions store recoverable credentials on behalf of their customers. A breach of the PAM vendor simultaneously exposes every customer's privileged credentials. DFS §500.11 requires equivalent controls on third-party access — a vendor that holds your complete credentials is itself a single point of failure that Part 500 is designed to eliminate.
High risk
MFA attested on application but not enforced for all privileged accounts
§500.12 requires MFA for all remote access and privileged access. Examiners are finding that attestations often do not reflect enforcement — legacy portals, service accounts, and emergency access paths are commonly excluded. Change Healthcare compromised 100M+ records through a single Citrix portal excluded from MFA. DFS examiners are now specifically probing for exceptions.
High risk
Shared admin accounts in use for privileged access
§500.7(b) explicitly prohibits shared admin accounts for privileged access. Examiners are finding shared accounts — particularly for vendor access, emergency procedures, and legacy system administration — that make it impossible to attribute privileged actions to specific individuals, as required by §500.14.
Emerging
Agentic AI systems with unilateral privileged access
AI agents deployed for automation inherit whatever credentials they are given and act on them without human oversight. §500.7 does not distinguish between human and non-human actors — privileged access by an AI agent is subject to the same least-privilege and attribution requirements. Standing privileged credentials held by AI agents are a gap that most covered entities have not yet addressed.
Emerging
Audit trails that depend on log aggregation rather than cryptographic proof
§500.6 requires audit trails that are reliable enough to detect and respond to cybersecurity events. Log-based audit trails can be altered, deleted, or incomplete. DFS examiners are beginning to distinguish between log aggregation and cryptographically bound audit records that are produced by the access mechanism itself and cannot be retroactively modified.
