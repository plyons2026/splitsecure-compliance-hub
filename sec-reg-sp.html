From privacy policies to verifiable technical safeguards
SEC Reg S-P — 2024 amendments
Firms remain accountable for the security, confidentiality, and integrity of customer information even when technology and services are outsourced. A vendor's notification promise is not a safeguard.
The SEC and FINRA have unified their emphasis on non-delegable accountability. The 2024 amendments move beyond privacy policies to require verifiable technical safeguards — an Incident Response Program with detection capabilities, a Service Provider Oversight framework with contractual notification requirements, and recordkeeping sufficient to support SEC examination. SplitSecure provides the architectural foundation for all three: protocol-level oversight of vendor access, cryptographic audit observations generated before any action executes, and no persistent credentials that could be improperly accessed or disclosed.
What creates Reg S-P exposure
A service provider with standing access to customer NPI who can act unilaterally. A 72-hour notification window that depends on the vendor's own detection and disclosure. Log-based audit trails that an attacker with admin rights can alter. Persistent credentials that remain accessible after an employee departure or system disposal.
Vendor-dependent accountability
What SplitSecure provides
No vendor holds standing access to customer NPI — every vendor action requires a live internal quorum from the firm's own devices. Unauthorized access attempts are visible in real time as proposals without quorum authorization. No persistent credential to improperly disclose. Cryptographic audit observations generated before any action executes.
Cryptographic sovereignty — non-delegable

Service provider rule — vendor oversight
Protocol-level oversight replacing the notification promise
Under the 2024 amendments, firms remain accountable for the security of customer NPI even when technology and services are outsourced. SplitSecure replaces contractual notification requirements with architectural controls — the firm sees vendor access in real time and authorizes it through a live internal quorum.
The ultimate accountability problem
SEC and FINRA unified guidance — 2024
Firms remain accountable for the security, confidentiality, and integrity of customer information even when technology and services are outsourced. A vendor's 72-hour notification promise is not a safeguard — it is a post-breach obligation.
The 72-hour notification requirement acknowledges that vendor breaches will happen. It does not prevent them. A service provider with standing access to customer NPI systems can act unilaterally — or be compromised and used to act unilaterally — without the firm's knowledge until the 72-hour clock starts. By then, customer NPI has already been exposed. SplitSecure eliminates standing vendor access entirely. Every vendor action requires a live internal quorum from the firm's own attested devices. The firm sees the vendor access request before it executes — not after the breach.
How SplitSecure enforces protocol-level vendor oversight
1
Vendor initiates a context-aware proposal
When a cloud host, custodian, or MSP needs to access a customer NPI system, they initiate a proposal specifying exactly what they need to do, on which system, and for how long. The proposal is visible to the firm's approval team before any access is granted. Nothing executes until the quorum is met.
Firm sees access request before it executes
2
Internal team reviews and authorizes on firm-controlled devices
The firm's own compliance or security team reviews the vendor proposal on hardware-attested devices and votes to authorize. Each vote is cryptographically signed by the approver's device. The vendor cannot self-authorize — the quorum must include internal firm parties on devices the firm controls.
Non-delegable — firm retains cryptographic sovereignty
3
Ephemeral access — scoped, time-limited, and fully logged
Once the quorum is met, the vendor receives ephemeral access scoped to the approved system and action. The credential is reconstructed inside an attested runtime for that operation and immediately discarded. The complete authorization chain is recorded as a tamper-evident cryptographic observation.
No standing vendor access — ever
4
Unauthorized attempts surface in real time
If a vendor attempts to access customer NPI systems without initiating a proposal — or initiates a proposal that fails to achieve quorum — the attempt is logged immediately in the firm's own audit trail. The firm does not wait for a 72-hour vendor notification. Detection is architectural.
Real-time detection — not vendor-dependent
Eliminating vendor single points of failure
The SPoF problem under Reg S-P
A service provider that holds standing credentials to customer NPI systems is a single point of failure. One breach of the provider simultaneously exposes every customer's data. Under the service provider rule, the firm is accountable for that breach — even if the firm had no visibility into the vendor's security practices.
Vendor-originated exposure
How SplitSecure eliminates the SPoF
Keys are split across the firm and the vendor — the vendor holds fragments, not complete credentials. A breach of the vendor's systems exposes key fragments insufficient to reconstruct the means of access. The firm's internal quorum participation is required for any access to customer NPI. The vendor is not a single point of failure.
SPoF eliminated architecturally
Incident response program
Detection and forensic integrity for the 30-day customer notification window
Reg S-P now mandates a written incident response program designed to detect and assess unauthorized access to customer information. Traditional text logs are often incomplete or easily modified — making assessment within the 30-day customer notification window unreliable. SplitSecure provides forensic-grade evidence generated by the access mechanism itself.
The detection and assessment problem
SEC Reg S-P — written incident response program
An incident response program that depends on log files an attacker with admin rights can alter is not a detection capability — it is a documentation exercise.
Traditional text-based audit logs are often the first thing an attacker deletes or modifies after gaining administrative access. An IRP that relies on these logs cannot reliably detect unauthorized access, assess its scope, or meet the 30-day customer notification window with confidence. SplitSecure's audit observations are generated by the access mechanism itself — before any action executes. They are SHA3-512 hashed, signed by a rotating signer, and chain-linked. An attacker with administrative rights cannot alter them without making the modification mathematically detectable.
What traditional IRPs rely on
Log files that an attacker with admin rights can alter, truncate, or delete. SIEM alerts that depend on the completeness of the underlying log data. Vendor notification that may arrive 72 hours after the breach. Point-in-time forensic analysis after the fact — often incomplete and contested.
Log-dependent — alterable
What SplitSecure provides
Cryptographic audit observations generated before any action executes. SHA3-512 hash chain — any modification is mathematically detectable. Unauthorized access attempts logged in real time as failed proposals. Per-account access log filterable for customer notification. Forensic-grade evidence available from the moment of the incident — not assembled after the fact.

02
Assessment — complete forensic reconstruction of any access event
Every access event includes the requesting identity, the approving devices and their hardware attestation proofs, the target system, the action taken, and the timestamp — all cryptographically bound. The scope of any unauthorized access is reconstructible from the audit record alone.
Evidence: cryptographic audit observation with full authorization chain per event

The audit trail is filterable by customer account, system, and date range. For any identified breach, the firm can generate a per-customer report of which accounts were accessed, when, and by whom — supporting the 30-day customer notification requirement with verifiable evidence.
Evidence: per-customer access log exportable for notification support

Evidence is available from day one — not assembled under examination pressure.
Evidence: SHA3-512 audit trail, hardware attestation proofs, SIEM-compatible export
