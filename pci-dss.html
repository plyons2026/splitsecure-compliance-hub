PCI DSS 4.0 — SplitSecure Compliance Hub
Architectural Integrity for the Cardholder Data Environment
PCI DSS 4.0 represents the most significant update to the standard in over a decade, shifting from point-in-time checks to a model of continuous security. QSAs now demand proof that access controls are technically enforced and that administrative actions are uniquely attributable. SplitSecure provides the mathematical foundation for these requirements, eliminating the shared credentials and single points of failure that traditional vaults leave behind.

From administrative discretion to protocol enforcement
PCI DSS 4.0 — continuous security model
PCI DSS 4.0 allows for "Customized Approaches" — but the core requirements for administrative access have tightened. Shared root passwords and legacy push MFA are high-risk findings in your next Report on Compliance.
The shift from version 3.2.1 to 4.0 reflects a broader regulatory movement: from documenting what should happen to proving what does happen. A QSA reviewing a CDE that relies on checked-out vault credentials, shared admin accounts, or push-based MFA is looking at a gap between stated controls and enforced ones. SplitSecure replaces administrative discretion with protocol enforcement — every action is cryptographically bound to a unique hardware-attested device, making individual accountability not a procedural goal but an architectural property.
What QSAs are flagging as high risk
Group IDs or shared passwords for administrative CDE access. Push-based or SMS MFA that can be bypassed through fatigue attacks. Vault credentials that can be checked out by a single user. Log-based audit trails that an attacker with admin rights can alter.
High-risk findings
What SplitSecure enforces architecturally
No shared credential can exist — every action requires a quorum of unique device signatures. No credential to phish — Shamir shares reconstructed ephemerally inside attested runtimes. No alterable log — the audit trail is a byproduct of the access mechanism itself.

Requirement 8.5 — shared credentials
Eliminating shared admin credentials from the CDE
Req 8.5.1 explicitly prohibits group IDs and shared passwords for administrative CDE functions. In traditional environments, vault-checked credentials make it impossible for QSAs to verify which individual performed a specific action. SplitSecure enforces cryptographic attribution — every action is signed by a unique, hardware-bound device.
The QSA's attribution problem
PCI DSS 4.0 Req 8.5.1
If "admin" credentials are checked out of a vault and used by multiple people, individual accountability is impossible. The QSA cannot determine which human performed which action within the CDE.
Traditional PAM solutions vault shared admin credentials and log check-outs. But once the credential is checked out, the vault has no visibility into what happens next. Multiple users may share the same credential simultaneously. The audit trail shows a credential was checked out — not which individual used it, what they did with it, or whether access was authorized. PCI DSS 4.0 requires individual accountability. SplitSecure provides it architecturally — every privileged action requires a quorum of unique device signatures, and each signature is cryptographically bound to a specific device and operator.
How SplitSecure enforces Req 8.5 architecturally
1
No complete credential exists to share
Shamir Secret Sharing distributes credential fragments across unique employee devices. No complete credential exists as a static object that can be checked out, shared, or stolen. There is nothing to put in a vault because there is nothing to vault.
Req 8.5.1 — no shared secrets by construction
2
Every action requires unique device signatures
To execute a privileged CDE action, a quorum of physical devices must cryptographically sign the request. Each signature is unique to the device and the operator. The QSA receives a verifiable chain of custody for every CDE action — not a check-out log.
Req 8.5.1 — unforgeable individual attribution
3
Ephemeral reconstruction — no standing privilege
When the quorum threshold is met, the secret is reconstructed ephemerally inside an attested runtime for that single operation and immediately discarded. No persistent credential is produced. There is no standing privilege that persists beyond the authorized action.
Req 7.2.2 — just-in-time access enforced

Requirement 8.4 — phishing-resistant MFA
Beyond push MFA — hardware-attested authentication for the CDE
PCI DSS 4.0 emphasizes the move toward phishing-resistant MFA. Legacy methods including SMS and simple push-to-approve are increasingly viewed as insufficient due to MFA fatigue and push bombing. SplitSecure eliminates the underlying credential entirely — there is nothing to phish, harvest, or replay.
Why push MFA is no longer sufficient
PCI DSS 4.0 Req 8.4 — multi-factor authentication
Push-based MFA protects the login event. It does not protect what a legitimately authenticated session can do in the CDE afterward — and it can be bypassed through MFA fatigue, push bombing, or deepfake impersonation.
The MGM breach demonstrated the practical limit of push-based MFA at scale. A help desk call bypassed MFA on a privileged account with a single social engineering interaction. PCI DSS 4.0's movement toward phishing-resistant authentication reflects this: the standard now favors asymmetric cryptography and hardware-bound authentication that eliminates the underlying credential rather than protecting access to it. SplitSecure satisfies this requirement and goes further — hardware attestation on every protocol message ensures the authorization request originated from a legitimate, unmodified device, not an emulated attacker script.
What push MFA cannot prevent
MFA fatigue — repeated push notifications until the user approves. Push bombing — flooding the device with approval requests. Social engineering — tricking a user or help desk into approving a malicious request. Session hijacking — using a legitimately authenticated session to take privileged actions after the login event.
Behaviorally dependent
What hardware attestation enforces
Every protocol message cryptographically bound to the identity and code of the hardware-attested runtime that produced it. A push notification cannot satisfy the quorum — the approval must originate from a legitimate, unmodified device. A phished user's click is one vote in a threshold that still requires independent authorization from separate attested devices.
Architecturally enforced
Three attack vectors — and why each fails
01
MFA fatigue and push bombing
Push-based MFA requires a user to click approve. SplitSecure requires a proactive, cryptographically signed vote from a hardware-attested runtime — not a button click. Repeated push notifications produce no valid attestation. The attack has no surface to operate on.
Hardware attestation certificate required per vote — reflexive clicks have no effect
02
Credential harvesting and replay attacks
FIDO2/WebAuthn passkeys eliminate the underlying password. There is no static credential to harvest, no token to replay, and no password to enter into a fake portal. Each proposal uses a fresh ephemeral key — an intercepted approval cannot be reused.
No persistent credential — nothing to harvest or replay
03
Help desk social engineering
SplitSecure requires a quorum of independent approvals from hardware-attested devices for every privileged CDE action — including any action that modifies authentication controls. A help desk call that resets MFA on a privileged account is itself a privileged action requiring quorum authorization. The MGM attack vector is architecturally eliminated.
Quorum required for MFA resets — single call cannot execute
Requirement 10 — audit and traceability
Self-auditing protocols — ROC-ready evidence by construction
Req 10 demands that audit trails be secured and capable of reconstructing all CDE administrative events. Traditional text-based logs are a high-risk liability — an attacker with administrative rights can alter or delete them. SplitSecure's audit trail is a byproduct of the access mechanism itself.
