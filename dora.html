From administrative trust to architectural resilience
DORA — EU 2022/2554
DORA differs from previous regulations by treating ICT risk as a function of business continuity. A single compromised vault or administrator is a resilience failure, not just a security incident.
Traditional compliance relies on administrative trust — the assumption that administrators will follow procedures, vendors will honour contractual commitments, and access controls documented in policy will be enforced in practice. DORA rejects this model. If a single administrator or a single compromised vault can bring down a critical financial service, the entity is non-compliant with DORA's resilience standards regardless of what the policy says. SplitSecure replaces administrative trust with architectural resilience — the k-of-n quorum model ensures that even if a device is destroyed or an admin is compromised, the integrity and availability of critical ICT functions remain intact.
What DORA identifies as non-compliant
A single administrator with sufficient access to disrupt critical services. A single vendor with standing access to sensitive systems. A PAM vault whose compromise simultaneously exposes all customer credentials. Log-based audit trails that an attacker with admin rights can alter or delete.
Single point of failure
What SplitSecure provides
No single device, admin, or vendor holds sufficient authority to act alone. Secrets reconstructed ephemerally inside attested runtimes for one operation, then discarded. Every action produces a tamper-evident cryptographic observation. The architectural condition that caused the CloudNordic total loss is structurally impossible.

Article 9 — separation of duties
Enforcing separation of duties at the protocol layer
DORA emphasizes that no single user should have excessive permissions that could lead to unauthorized changes to critical systems. Traditional role-based access control fails when an account is hijacked or an insider goes rogue. SplitSecure enforces cryptographic separation of duties — not procedural.
The problem with over-privileged users
DORA Article 9 — ICT security policies
Traditional RBAC fails when an account is hijacked or an insider goes rogue. The permission exists. The protection is behavioral, not architectural.
Role-based access control assigns permissions based on job function. It documents what users should be able to do and under what conditions. It does not prevent a compromised account from exercising those permissions, an insider from abusing legitimate access, or a help desk operator from resetting controls that other controls depend on. DORA's resilience standard requires that no single failure — human or technical — can disrupt critical services. That standard cannot be satisfied by RBAC alone.
What RBAC cannot prevent
A compromised account exercising its legitimate permissions. An insider with the correct role taking a destructive action. A help desk operator resetting MFA on a privileged account without independent authorization. A single admin rotating production keys without a second approver. Any of these produces a DORA resilience failure.
Behaviorally dependent
What threshold cryptography enforces
A compromised account cannot act alone — it is one vote in a quorum that still requires independent authorization from separate attested devices. An insider with legitimate access cannot execute a privileged action without a second independent approval. The separation of duties is enforced at the protocol layer, not documented in a policy.
Architecturally enforced
How the propose-vote-execute model works
1
Propose
An administrator initiates a privileged action — for example, rotating database keys, modifying firewall rules, or granting vendor access. The proposal is context-aware: it specifies who is requesting, what system is targeted, what action is requested, and for how long. The action is locked until the quorum threshold is met.
Action-level resolution — not just login
2
Vote
A minimum threshold of independent approvers review the proposal on their own hardware-attested devices. Each vote is a cryptographic signature bound to the device identity and the specific proposal. No approver can vote on behalf of another. A hardware-level compromise of one device is insufficient to meet the quorum.
Hardware-attested — replay-proof
3
Execute
Once the quorum threshold is met, the secret is reconstructed ephemerally inside an attested runtime for that single operation, then immediately discarded. The execution event is recorded as a tamper-evident cryptographic observation. No persistent credential is ever produced.
Ephemeral reconstruction — discarded immediately
What the quorum model protects against
Insider threat
A rogue insider with legitimate credentials cannot execute a privileged action alone. Their approval is one vote in a threshold. The action does not execute without independent authorization from separate devices controlled by separate individuals.
Compromised account
A stolen or phished account credential cannot authorize unilateral action. The account is one approver in a quorum. Even if the attacker has full control of that account, the action requires additional independent approvals from hardware-attested devices they do not control.
Social engineering
An attacker who tricks an administrator into approving a malicious request — through deepfake impersonation, MFA fatigue, or help desk manipulation — gains one vote in a quorum that still requires independent authorization from devices the attacker has not compromised.
Single admin availability risk
The quorum model also protects against unavailability. If one approver is unreachable, the threshold can be met by the remaining quorum members — without relying on a single administrator whose absence blocks critical operations.
Articles 28–30 — ICT third-party risk
Accountability cannot be delegated
Under DORA, financial entities remain fully accountable for their ICT risk even when services are outsourced to critical third-party providers. SplitSecure gives financial entities cryptographic sovereignty over their own systems — no vendor holds a complete credential, and no vendor can act without internal authorization.
The third-party access problem
DORA Articles 28–30 — ICT third-party risk management
Handing a static service account or long-lived API key to a managed service provider is a DORA compliance violation if they can act unilaterally on your systems.
Traditional MSP arrangements grant vendors standing access to customer systems through service accounts, API keys, or PAM credentials managed on the vendor's infrastructure. Under DORA, the financial entity remains fully accountable for ICT risk regardless of contractual terms. A vendor that holds complete credentials and can act unilaterally is a single point of failure — one breach of the MSP simultaneously exposes every customer's systems. SplitSecure eliminates this by ensuring vendors never hold a complete credential and cannot act without a live internal quorum from the financial entity's own devices.
How SplitSecure secures the supply chain
1
Vendor initiates a proposal
The MSP or third-party provider submits a context-aware proposal specifying the system, the action, and the time window required. The proposal is visible to the financial entity's approval team before any access is granted. Nothing executes until the quorum is met.
2
Internal team reviews and approves
The financial entity's own team reviews the proposal on their hardware-attested devices. Each approval is cryptographically signed and bound to the approver's device identity. The vendor's proposal cannot be self-approved — the quorum must include internal parties.
Cryptographic sovereignty — internal approval required
3
Ephemeral access — scoped, time-limited, context-aware
Once the quorum is met, the secret is reconstructed ephemerally for that single operation and immediately discarded. The vendor never "holds" the key. Access is scoped to the specific system and action requested. The complete audit record is generated as a byproduct of the execution.
No standing vendor credentials — ever
What DORA examiners can verify
No vendor holds a complete credential
Architectural attestation confirms SplitSecure's infrastructure cannot reconstruct keys or act on customer systems. The vendor supply chain dependency — the single point of failure DORA is designed to eliminate — is architecturally removed.
Art. 28 — Verified
Every vendor action required internal authorization
Third-party access report shows every vendor-originated event with the internal quorum approval that authorized it. No vendor action appears in the audit trail without a corresponding internal approval from the financial entity's own attested devices.
Art. 30 — Verified
Access was scoped and time-limited
Every vendor proposal specifies scope, target system, and duration. The audit record confirms execution was bounded to the approved parameters. No standing vendor access persists beyond the authorized window.
Art. 29 — Verified
Concentration risk is identifiable and auditable
Vendor access is filterable by provider across any time window. Dependency concentration across critical systems is visible in the third-party access report — enabling the concentration risk assessment DORA Article 30 requires.
Art. 30 — Verified
Article 18 — audit and forensic evidence
Self-auditing protocols — tamper-evident by construction
DORA requires systems to monitor, manage, and log ICT-related incidents to evaluate attacks and report to authorities. Traditional logs are often the first thing an attacker deletes. SplitSecure's audit trail is a byproduct of the access mechanism itself — not a separate log that can be removed.

Why this satisfies Art. 18
The record is produced by the access mechanism itself — not a separate logging layer. An attacker with admin rights cannot alter it without invalidating the SHA3-512 hash. It contains sufficient information to forensically reconstruct any privileged action for regulatory submission.
What cannot be fabricated
Each device signature is independently verifiable using the device's public key. The observation hash chains to the previous record. A record cannot be inserted or removed from the sequence without breaking the chain — mathematically detectable by DORA examiners.
