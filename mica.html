Hardening the "Means of Access" Under MiCA
MiCA represents a fundamental shift in crypto-asset governance, moving from unregulated operations to a regime of strict institutional resilience. Under Articles 70 and 75, CASPs are strictly liable for the loss of crypto-assets or the means of access resulting from hacks or malfunctions. SplitSecure provides the mathematical foundation to eliminate the single points of failure that trigger this liability.

From centralized custody to distributed sovereignty
MiCA — EU 2023/1114, Articles 70 and 75
If a single employee, a single server, or a single HSM represents a path to total asset exfiltration, the CASP is carrying an unmitigated concentration risk that regulators explicitly flag under strict liability.
Traditional crypto-asset custody relies on a single root access path — a master key, an HSM, or an admin account that holds sufficient authority to move client assets unilaterally. Under MiCA Articles 70 and 75, the CASP is strictly liable if that path is exploited through a hack or malfunction. SplitSecure replaces centralized custody with distributed sovereignty — the means of access is split into n fragments via Shamir Secret Sharing. A complete private key never exists as a static object. Reconstructing it requires a k-of-n mathematical proof across independent hardware-attested devices, engineering the malfunction or hack liability out of the architecture.
What creates strict liability exposure
A master key or HSM that holds complete signing authority for client assets. A single admin with sufficient access to move funds unilaterally. A warm wallet provider whose compromise simultaneously exposes all client assets. Any architecture where one failure point — human or technical — produces total asset exfiltration.
Concentration risk — Art. 75 liability
What SplitSecure eliminates
No complete private key ever exists as a static object. Reconstruction requires a k-of-n mathematical proof across independent hardware-attested devices. No single device, employee, sub-custodian, or SplitSecure itself holds sufficient key material to move client assets. The architectural condition for Art. 75 liability is removed.

Article 75 — means of access
Eliminating the single path to total asset exfiltration
Regulators, including the EBA and ESMA, require CASPs to demonstrate they are not concentrating risk with a single provider or a single internal admin. Traditional warm or cold storage often still relies on a single root access path that can be phished, socially engineered, or malfunctioned. SplitSecure enforces threshold signing authority — no nuclear master key, no single path to total loss.
The concentration of risk problem
MiCA Article 75 — custody policy
Traditional custody arrangements rely on a single "root" access path — a master key, a warm wallet, or an HSM that holds complete signing authority. Under MiCA Art. 75, that path is a strict liability risk.
EBA and ESMA guidance on MiCA implementation consistently flags concentration of custody risk as the primary concern. A CASP that relies on a single HSM, a single cloud key management service, or a single admin account with sufficient authority to move client assets is carrying an unmitigated Art. 75 liability — regardless of what the custody policy document says. SplitSecure eliminates this by ensuring no complete private key ever exists. Reconstructing the means of access requires a k-of-n proof across independent hardware-attested devices. The architectural condition for total asset exfiltration is removed.
How SplitSecure secures the means of access
1
No nuclear master key — distributed fragments across independent devices
Private keys are split across a group of independent hardware-attested devices using Shamir Secret Sharing. No single device holds sufficient key material to reconstruct the means of access. The CASP, SplitSecure, sub-custodians, and cloud infrastructure each hold fragments — none holds a complete key.
Art. 75 — no single path to exfiltration
2
Action-level gating — every transaction is a multi-party event
You do not just gate the login to a custodial portal — you gate the specific signing event. Every asset movement requires the team to collectively confirm that the transaction complies with the CASP's internal policy. Shareholders review exactly what they are signing — address, amount, destination — on their physical devices before casting a vote.
Art. 68 — context-aware voting
3
Ephemeral reconstruction — means of access discarded after each use
Once the quorum threshold is met, the key is reconstructed ephemerally inside an attested runtime for that single signing event and immediately discarded. No persistent means of access is produced. A successful signing event leaves no recoverable key material — for an attacker or for the CASP itself.
Art. 75 — malfunction liability eliminated
4
Client cryptographic sovereignty — client participation in quorum
The client retains at least one device in the quorum configuration. SplitSecure and the CASP cannot reconstruct the client's keys without the client's own attested device participating in the vote. Custody concentration risk is distributed — not simply transferred to a different provider.
Art. 70 — no single-provider concentration

Article 68 — governance and internal controls
Eliminating the rogue insider — cryptographic separation of duties
MiCA Article 68 demands that management bodies possess appropriate knowledge and that internal control mechanisms ensure the integrity of the CASP. SplitSecure replaces policy promises with cryptographic enforcement — governance does not depend on the repute of any individual.
Why governance-by-reputation fails
MiCA Article 68 — internal controls
Regulators require CASPs to demonstrate internal control mechanisms that ensure operational integrity. A policy that says "only authorized personnel may move client assets" is not a mechanism — it is a promise.
MiCA's internal controls requirements reflect a broader regulatory movement away from governance based on the repute of individuals and toward governance based on technical constraint. An admin who is "highly reputable" can still scrape keys from their own device memory if the system permits it. An insider who is "trusted" can still move client assets unilaterally if no second approver is required. SplitSecure replaces these behavioral dependencies with cryptographic enforcement — operator opacity means even a legitimate admin cannot access key material from their own device memory, and threshold quorums mean no individual can execute a custodial action without multi-party authorization.
How SplitSecure hardens governance
Immutable delegation graph
Authorization is expressed as a signed statement from one identity to another — cryptographically provable chains of authority replacing traditional access control lists. Every delegation is recorded, timestamped, and unforgeable. Regulators can trace the complete authorization chain for any custodial action.
Art. 68 — Verifiable
Context-aware voting — shareholders see what they sign
Quorum members review the exact transaction on their physical devices before casting a vote — destination address, asset amount, counterparty. There is no blind approval. The voter must confirm the specific custodial action, not just authenticate their identity.
Art. 68 — Verifiable
Operator opacity — memory is opaque to the device operator
System memory is opaque to the device operator or OS during key reconstruction. Even a legitimate admin running the SplitSecure software on their own hardware cannot scrape key fragments from device memory. Physical device possession does not grant access to key material — the quorum threshold must be met.
Art. 14 — Verifiable
Hardware attestation on every vote
Every vote in the quorum is cryptographically bound to the identity and code of the hardware-attested runtime that produced it. An emulated or tampered device cannot produce a valid attestation. Governance is enforced at the hardware layer — not the policy layer.
Art. 14 — Verifiable
The rogue insider scenario — why it fails against SplitSecure
01
Insider with legitimate access attempts unilateral asset movement
The insider's device holds one fragment of the key. Initiating a signing proposal requires a quorum. The proposal is visible to all quorum members before any vote is cast. The asset movement does not execute without independent authorization from devices controlled by separate individuals. The attempt is logged as a proposal with no quorum achieved.
Evidence: failed quorum record; proposal log without execution
02
Insider attempts to extract key material from their own device
System memory is opaque during key reconstruction — the operator and OS cannot read key fragments during the signing process. The fragment on the device is a Shamir share, not the complete key. Possession of one fragment is mathematically insufficient to reconstruct the means of access.
Evidence: hardware attestation confirming operator-opaque runtime
03
Compromised sub-custodian or cloud provider attempts to move assets
Sub-custodian and cloud infrastructure hold key fragments — not complete keys. The client's own attested device participates in the quorum. A compromise of the sub-custodian or cloud provider is insufficient to reconstruct the means of access without client device participation. The architectural condition for Art. 75 liability does not arise.
Evidence: quorum configuration confirming client device requirement; third-party custody dependency map
